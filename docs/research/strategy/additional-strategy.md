1. Solo and 2p cannot cancel immunity at all — there are zero immunity-Jesters. This is the biggest one for a solo-first game. The two solo Jesters are lives (discard-and-refill), and the rules explicitly say flipping them that way does not cancel immunity. The tavern Jester count is 1p=0, 2p=0, 3p=1, 4p=2 — so the entire "spend the Jester to unblock the lever-King" strategy is a 3p/4p-only tool. In 70% of your audience, the Spade King's blocked shields and the Diamond King's blocked draw simply cannot be unblocked — you beat them raw or you die. That makes an immunity-cancel effect (a class power, a relic) disproportionately, almost uniquely valuable in solo, which is real design space you currently have nothing in.
2. Matched low cards are the most lever-dense plays in the game — they are not junk. A same-rank combo fires every suit's power at the combined total. The hidden gem: a quad of 2s (2♦2♥2♣2♠ = 8) is the only play that fires all four levers at once — draw 8, recover 8, shield 8, double damage, minus whichever one the enemy blocks. Triples fire three levers, pairs fire two. So your earlier "2s and 3s always discard" is only true for lone low cards; matched low cards are premium multi-lever turns. This inverts the junk tier and opens a design knob: anything that helps you assemble sets (draw toward matches, hold pairs) is secretly a power-lever, and a class built on "pair cards" (your earlier idea) is mechanically native, not bolted on.
3. Max hand size is the master lever, because it's three budgets in one card count. It's your ammo, your discard-payment ceiling (survival), and your draw headroom simultaneously. A boss that shrinks it doesn't apply one pressure — it strangles offense, defense, and refill at the same time, which is why your instinct that it'd make a great pressure-point is dead right. The inverse is just as strong: +1 max hand size is one of the most overpowered buffs you could hand out, so it's a knob to guard carefully.
4. The Jester is a fight-long lever restoration with a sequencing tech, not a one-card unlock. Cancelling immunity persists for the whole enemy and works retroactively — spades you played before the Jester suddenly start shielding. But clubs are the explicit exception: clubs played before the Jester do not retroactively double. So there's real tech here: against the Spade King, dump your spades first (dormant), then Jester to light them all up at once; against the Club King, Jester first, then play clubs so they double. The bot (and a skilled player) sequences around that asymmetry.
5. Recruited royals are dual-purpose bombs. A King recruited to hand is both a 20-damage suit-power card and a single card that pays a full King's counterattack. King of Diamonds played (against a non-Diamond enemy) draws 20 — the biggest refill in the game; held, it's 20 points of survival armor. So precision recruiting sculpts your offense and raises your survival ceiling, and there's a sequencing rule: never play a recruited royal into the enemy that blocks its suit.
6. The ♥→♦ pipeline is the only escape from an empty tavern — and its absence is the terminal death spiral. Once the tavern is dry, draw fizzles harmlessly but does nothing; Hearts are the only way to put cards back (to the bottom), and then Diamonds reach them. No Heart access + dry tavern = your hand only shrinks until you can't pay a hit. That's the loss condition under the hood, and it's why a boss that blocks Hearts (the Heart King) or thins your deck is attacking your resurrection button, not just an economy.
7. Two smaller corners worth knowing. Big draws are wasted at high hand size (you fizzle at the cap), so draw timing has diminishing returns — a 10-draw at 5/8 nets you 3, which means a hand-size-shrinking boss also throttles your draw efficiency, compounding lever #3. And over-shielding is dead value: a fully-shielded enemy with empty hands is the stall corner (and a known engine soft-lock) — which is the degenerate line your attrition mechanics need to keep punishing.
The through-line under all of these: the game is really a four-lever economy where every enemy disables exactly one lever, the Jester restores one (except in solo), hand size is the shared budget, and matched low cards are the densest way to fire multiple levers per card spent. Your churn-vs-survival tension is the visible surface of that; the hidden depth is that low matched cards and recruited royals let skilled play break the "low = weak, high = strong" intuition entirely.
